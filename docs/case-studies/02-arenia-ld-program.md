# Case study 02 — L&D program ROI diagnosis, B2B training provider

> **[DRAFT — anonymized, pending final user verification of metrics.]** Based on a real Arenia client engagement. Sector and order-of-magnitude details preserved; identifying data obscured.

- **Date:** 2026 Q1
- **Sector:** B2B sales training provider (corporate L&D)
- **Client size:** ~200 trainers in the network · ~3,000 learners per year · top 3 enterprise accounts representing ~60% of revenue
- **Project type:** strategy + learning-operations hybrid
- **Outcome:** measurable competence improvement demonstrated on a pilot cohort; top-account contract renewed with expansion clause.

---

## Context

A mid-sized training provider specialized in sales negotiation and customer-service soft skills for large enterprise accounts. The business model is classic: cohorts of 12-20 learners, one or two in-person workshops per year, trainer-led role-play exercises. Pricing is per-cohort per-workshop.

In Q4 2025 the provider lost a renewal pitch for its second-largest account. The account's procurement team had adopted a new L&D vendor-evaluation framework that required demonstrable behavioral KPI retention at 30 / 60 / 90 days. The provider had no data to answer the question.

The risk: the top account's contract was up for renewal in Q2 2026. Same evaluation framework. Same question.

The provider's L&D director approached Arenia. The brief was: "help us prove that our training works, in the vocabulary the procurement team uses, in time for the Q2 review".

Arenia ran `autogap` on the curriculum bundle the provider shared:

- The sales-negotiation training curriculum (3 modules, 12 role-play scenarios).
- Instructor post-workshop notes from the last 18 months (anonymized, ~120 pages).
- Post-training survey data (1,400 responses over 2 years).
- The procurement evaluation framework used by the lost account (2 pages).
- Historical KPI data the provider had collected (not structured, scattered across workshop reports).

## Objective hypothesis, inferred

> Demonstrate measurable behavioral retention on the negotiation module for the top enterprise account, in a format that passes their L&D procurement evaluation, in time for the Q2 2026 contract review.

Confidence: **High.**

Strongest alternative: "Reinvent the training product across all modules" — attractive long-term, but out of scope for the Q2 deadline. The deadline was the forcing function.

## Top 3 gaps

### Gap 1 — No behavioral retention measurement after the workshop ends

The provider collected post-workshop satisfaction (Kirkpatrick Level 1) and some Level 2 quiz results. Nothing at Level 3 (behavior change) or Level 4 (business impact). The procurement framework the account used scored heavily on Levels 3 and 4. Without those measurements, the entire conversation was uphill regardless of training quality.

### Gap 2 — Static scenarios produce diminishing returns after the workshop

The 12 role-play scenarios were fixed. Learners practiced them during the workshop and had no access to fresh practice afterwards. Research on spaced practice for behavioral retention is strong: without repeated low-stakes practice, content retention drops ~70% in 24 hours. The provider was essentially selling a leaky bucket.

### Gap 3 — Instructor dependency limits scale and consistency

Every cohort needed a trainer. Availability bottlenecked delivery; trainer skill variance bottlenecked consistency. For the top account (multi-region, multi-language), this made any quality-vs-volume comparison a coin flip and gave procurement the easy "how do you guarantee consistency at scale?" objection.

## 3 macro-steps

### Macro-Step 1 — Arenia pilot on negotiation module, 4 months

Deploy Arenia's Partner Start pilot on one cohort from the top account. Configure 3 negotiation scenarios mirroring the existing curriculum. Measure competence on baseline + every 30 days.

**Complexity:** Medium · **Value:** High · **Confidence:** High.

### Macro-Step 2 — Custom KPI framework aligned to procurement language

Build a 1-page KPI framework that translates the procurement team's vocabulary (retention, transfer, application, business outcome) into measurements the Arenia platform already produces. Use it as the reporting artifact delivered to the account monthly.

**Complexity:** Low · **Value:** High · **Confidence:** High.

### Macro-Step 3 — Scenario library expansion with customer-specific contexts

Take the top account's actual customer types, deal sizes, and objection patterns. Produce 6 new Arenia scenarios tailored to their real sales motion. Available 24/7 for continued practice.

**Complexity:** Medium · **Value:** Medium · **Confidence:** Medium.

## Recommended execution order

1 and 2 in parallel → 3.

The pilot (1) generates the data that the KPI framework (2) reports on — they must be designed together to ensure the framework measures what the pilot produces. The scenario library (3) is the expansion path and depends on the pilot having shown measurable results first (so the account funds it).

## What happened

Arenia's Partner Start pilot deployed in 4 weeks. The KPI framework was co-designed with the client's L&D director in parallel. First monthly report landed with the procurement team on schedule.

Measured competence on the pilot cohort (n=18) showed a 32% improvement on the three negotiation scenarios, measured on baseline-vs-month-4 performance. Retention (scored at 90 days) held.

The scenario library expansion followed in month 4-5. The account signed a renewal with an expansion clause covering two additional modules, each to be scoped against the same framework.

## What `autogap` did well

- **Rejected the "reinvent the product" frame.** Two earlier internal proposals had been scoped as full-product redesigns (~18 months). `autogap` anchored on the Q2 deadline and produced a 4-month scope that was executable.
- **Recognized the procurement framework as the real objective-setter.** The client had treated it as a document to respond to, not as a spec for internal design. `autogap`'s objective hypothesis made that spec explicit.

## What we learned

- For behavioral-training ROI, the measurement framework is the product. The workshop quality matters less than whether the buyer can tell that it mattered.
- Translating customer-procurement vocabulary into vendor-side measurement is often the largest single trust-transfer lever in B2B renewals.
